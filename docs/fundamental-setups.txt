GORDON'S 8 FUNDAMENTAL SETUPS — Explained With Examples
=========================================================

These are NOT "buy this type of company" rules. These are SITUATIONS
that cause a stock's fundamentals to change. Gordon looks for these
AFTER the technical signals (volume spike + breakout) have already
flagged the stock. The setup answers the question: "WHY is this
stock moving?"


SETUP 1: BUSINESS MIX CHANGE
------------------------------

What it means:
A company shifts its revenue from a low-growth or low-margin business
to a high-growth or high-margin one.

Example:
Imagine a company that makes 80% of its revenue from selling basic
steel pipes (5% margin) and 20% from selling specialty stainless steel
pipes for pharma companies (25% margin).

Over 2-3 years, the specialty business grows to 50% of revenue.
Total revenue might be the same, but profits double because the
mix shifted to the higher-margin product.

The stock has been consolidating because nobody noticed — revenue
didn't change. But margins are quietly expanding. One quarter the
profit jumps 40% and the market suddenly wakes up.

Real-world parallel: Think of a company like Deepak Nitrite — started
as a basic chemical company, shifted mix to value-added specialty
chemicals. Stock went from 200 to 2000+ as the market recognized
the mix change.

How to spot it: Quarter-on-quarter segment revenue breakdown.
If a high-margin segment is growing faster than the overall
company, the mix is shifting.


SETUP 2: MANAGEMENT CHANGE
-----------------------------

What it means:
New CEO, new promoter, or new management team comes in. Market gets
hopeful that things will change.

Example:
A company has been poorly run for years. Promoter's son takes over,
or a professional CEO is hired from a successful company. Even
before any results show up, the SENTIMENT changes. Investors think
"maybe now things will improve." The stock starts moving on hope.

The smart play is: don't buy on the management change announcement
alone. Wait for the volume spike + breakout to confirm that serious
money agrees with the hope. Then check if the new management is
actually doing anything different — cutting costs, launching new
products, reducing debt.

Real-world parallel: Think of Tata Motors when they brought focus
back on Indian business and EVs. Or any company where a known
turnaround specialist takes charge.

How to spot it: News, exchange filings, board meeting announcements.
This is one where Google/AI news scanning helps.


SETUP 3: SECTOR OF THE CYCLE
-------------------------------

What it means:
An entire sector starts doing well because of macro/fundamental
reasons. The market starts treating all companies in that sector
as winners.

Example:
Government announces a massive infrastructure spending plan.
First, L&T moves (the leader). Then mid-cap infra companies.
Then small-cap contractors. Then building material companies.
Then cement companies. The money cascades down.

Investors who can't buy L&T (too expensive, already run up)
start buying the "followers" — smaller companies in the same
sector. This is called "relative valuation" — if L&T trades
at 30x PE, then this smaller infra company at 15x PE looks
"cheap" in comparison.

Gordon's examples from the document:
- Telecom in late 90s
- IT services in 2000s
- Infrastructure in mid 2000s
- Consumer stocks in late 2000s
- Pharma in mid 2010s
- NBFCs in mid 2010s
- Chemical stocks in late 2010s
- Tech stocks during Covid
- Capital goods post Covid
- PSU stocks in 2023
- Solar stocks in 2024

ALL OF THEM STARTED AND ALL OF THEM ENDED.

The game: Get in when the sector is showing early signs of
strength (volume + breakouts across multiple stocks in the
sector). Get out when the exit signals appear (covered later).

How to spot it: Sector indices showing relative strength.
Multiple stocks in the same sector triggering volume spikes
and breakouts simultaneously.


SETUP 4: FORCED BUYING OR SELLING
------------------------------------

What it means:
Someone is buying or selling NOT because of the company's
fundamentals, but because they HAVE TO.

FORCED BUYING examples:
- Index inclusion: When a stock gets added to Nifty 50 or
  Nifty Next 50, every ETF and index fund that tracks that
  index MUST buy the stock. Regardless of price. That's
  billions of rupees of forced buying.
- Sector index inclusion: Same thing at sector level.

FORCED SELLING examples:
- A mutual fund manager's strategy says "only large caps."
  A stock drops out of large-cap classification. They MUST
  sell. Not because the company is bad — because the rules
  say so.
- A promoter gets arrested for a personal reason (tax raid,
  family dispute). Institutional investors panic-sell because
  of compliance rules. Company fundamentals haven't changed.
- A fund is shutting down or facing redemptions. They sell
  their best stocks because those have the most liquidity.
  Not because the stocks are bad — because they need cash.

Why this is a setup:
Forced selling creates artificially depressed prices. The
stock drops not because the business got worse but because
someone was forced to dump shares. If you can identify this,
you're buying a good company at a discount created by
someone else's problem.

How to spot it: Index rebalancing announcements (NSE publishes
these), news about promoter issues, mutual fund portfolio
disclosures, bulk deal data on exchanges.


SETUP 5: SUDDEN AND BIG EARNINGS SURPRISE
--------------------------------------------

What it means:
A company reports quarterly results that are WAY above what
anyone expected. Not 10% better — 50-100% better.

Example:
A mid-cap pharma company has been doing Rs 50 crore profit
per quarter for 2 years. Suddenly they report Rs 120 crore.
What happened? Maybe a new drug got approved, maybe they won
a big contract, maybe raw material costs crashed.

The momentum crowd (traders, algo funds, social media) jumps
in immediately. The stock can move 30-100% in days.

But here's the key question Gordon asks: is this SUSTAINABLE
or a one-time thing?

- One-time insurance claim boosted profit? Not sustainable.
  Skip.
- New product line that will keep generating revenue?
  Sustainable. Dig deeper.
- Commodity price crash reduced costs? Check if the price
  crash will last.

How to spot it: Quarterly results on exchanges. Compare
current quarter profit vs same quarter last year AND vs
previous quarter. A massive jump = earnings surprise.
Screener.in shows this data clearly.


SETUP 6: DEBT REDUCTION
--------------------------

What it means:
A company is paying off its debt significantly. Gordon says
"Debt is the only real number on a balance sheet" — meaning
revenue and profit can be window-dressed with accounting
tricks, but debt is hard to fake. If it's going down,
real cash is being generated or infused.

Example:
A company has Rs 500 crore of debt. Over 3 quarters, it
drops to Rs 300 crore. That Rs 200 crore came from somewhere —
either the business is generating serious cash flow, or a
new investor put money in.

Either way, something has changed:
- Less debt = less interest payment = higher profits going forward
- Less debt = less risk = market gives higher valuation multiple
- Less debt = more cash available for growth

This is the "quietest" signal because debt reduction doesn't
make headlines. Revenue growth does. But debt reduction is
often more meaningful.

Real-world parallel: Think of companies post-IBC resolution
(Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code). Companies like Tata Steel
acquiring Bhushan Steel — massive debt was restructured.
The stock re-rated significantly as debt came under control.

How to spot it: Compare total debt across last 4-6 quarters
on Screener.in. A consistent downward trend is the signal.


SETUP 7: BALANCE SHEET IMPROVEMENT
-------------------------------------

What it means:
The company is using its assets and capital more efficiently.
Not just debt reduction — but overall getting leaner and meaner.

The two key metrics:
- Asset turnover: Revenue divided by total assets. If this is
  going up, the company is squeezing more revenue from the same
  assets. Like a restaurant serving more customers with the same
  kitchen.
- Working capital improvement: Less money stuck in inventory,
  faster collection from customers, better management of
  supplier payments. This RELEASES CASH without the company
  needing to borrow or raise money.

Example:
A manufacturing company used to keep 90 days of inventory.
New management brings it down to 45 days. That's months of
production cost freed up as cash. The P&L might look similar
but the cash flow statement shows a big jump. And ROCE
(Return on Capital Employed) improves — which is what
sophisticated investors look at.

How to spot it: ROCE trending upwards over quarters.
Working capital days (inventory + receivable - payable days)
going down. Cash flow from operations improving even if
revenue is flat.


SETUP 8: SUPPLY DISRUPTIONS
------------------------------

What it means:
Something happens that reduces the supply of a product.
Demand stays the same or increases. Prices shoot up.
Companies in that industry make super-normal profits.

Example:
China shuts down chemical factories for environmental
reasons. India is the next biggest producer. Suddenly,
Indian chemical companies are getting orders at 2-3x
the normal price. Their costs haven't changed but their
selling price has doubled. Profits explode.

Or: A cyclone hits the coast and disrupts shipping routes.
Freight rates spike. Shipping companies that were barely
profitable suddenly report record earnings.

Or: Russia-Ukraine war disrupts gas supply. European
energy prices spike. Any company that produces or trades
energy makes windfall profits.

The catch: Supply disruptions are usually TEMPORARY.
The super-normal profits last 2-6 quarters. Then supply
normalizes, new capacity comes online, prices fall, and
the party is over. Gordon would ride this wave but watch
closely for exit signals.

How to spot it: Commodity price spikes, news about factory
shutdowns or natural disasters affecting production, trade
restrictions or sanctions. Cross-reference with which Indian
companies benefit from that specific commodity.


HOW GORDON USES THESE SETUPS
===============================

He's NOT scanning for all 8 at all times. The process is:

1. Volume spike + breakout flags a stock (technical filter)
2. He asks: "WHY is this moving?"
3. He checks if the answer fits one of these 8 setups
4. If yes — the move has fundamental backing. Dig deeper.
5. If no — the move might be speculation. Be cautious.

The setups also tell him HOW the story might end:
- Earnings surprise → check if it's sustainable or one-time
- Supply disruption → usually temporary, watch for normalization
- Sector of the cycle → all cycles end, watch for sector exhaustion
- Forced selling → temporary dip, should recover once selling stops

That's what he means by "boundary conditions" — the setup tells
you not just why it started, but gives clues about when it might end.
