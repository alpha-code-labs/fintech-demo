DISCOUNTED CASH FLOWS (DCF) — Explained Simply
================================================

Imagine you have a friend who says: "I'll give you Rs 1,000 one year from now."

Is that Rs 1,000 worth Rs 1,000 to you TODAY? No. Because if you had Rs 1,000 today,
you could put it in a bank and earn interest. In a year, it would be Rs 1,060 (at 6% interest).

So Rs 1,000 coming one year later is actually worth only about Rs 943 today.
That's called the "present value" of future money.

WHAT IS DCF?
------------
DCF is a way to figure out what a company is worth TODAY by looking at all the money
it will make in the FUTURE and asking: "What is all that future money worth right now?"

Step 1: Estimate how much cash the company will generate every year for the next
        5, 10, or 20 years.

Step 2: "Discount" each year's cash — meaning reduce its value because money later
        is worth less than money now.

Step 3: Add up all those discounted amounts. That total is what the company is
        "intrinsically" worth today.

SIMPLE EXAMPLE
--------------
A chai stall makes Rs 1,00,000 profit per year. You expect it to run for 5 years.

Year 1: Rs 1,00,000 --> discounted to Rs 94,300 today
Year 2: Rs 1,00,000 --> discounted to Rs 89,000 today
Year 3: Rs 1,00,000 --> discounted to Rs 83,900 today
Year 4: Rs 1,00,000 --> discounted to Rs 79,200 today
Year 5: Rs 1,00,000 --> discounted to Rs 74,700 today

Total (DCF value) = Rs 4,21,100

So this chai stall is "worth" about Rs 4.2 lakhs today. If someone is selling it
for Rs 3 lakhs — it's a bargain. If they want Rs 6 lakhs — it's overpriced.

THE "DISCOUNT RATE"
-------------------
The percentage you use to reduce future money is called the discount rate.
It basically answers: "What return could I get if I put my money somewhere else instead?"

- If bank gives 6%, your discount rate is at least 6%
- If you want 15% return (because stocks are risky), you use 15%
- Higher discount rate = future cash is worth LESS today = company is worth LESS

WHY GORDON SAYS DCF IS BROKEN
------------------------------
Gordon's point: the discount rate used to be somewhat stable and predictable.
But now:

- Governments print money (low interest rates mess up the discount rate)
- Foreign money flows in and out of India (capital flows change valuations overnight)
- SIP investors and mutual funds HAVE to invest money every month regardless of
  valuations (forced buying pushes prices above what DCF says they're worth)

So a stock might be "worth" Rs 500 by DCF, but trades at Rs 900 because there's
too much money chasing too few good stocks. The DCF says it's overpriced.
The market doesn't care.

That's why Gordon says relying only on fundamental analysis (DCF) is like
"working with half the toolkit."
