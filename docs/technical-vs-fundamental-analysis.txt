TECHNICAL ANALYSIS vs FUNDAMENTAL ANALYSIS — Explained Simply
==============================================================

These are the two main schools of thinking in investing/trading worldwide.
Every investor uses one or both. Gordon uses both.


FUNDAMENTAL ANALYSIS — "Is this company worth buying?"
======================================================

You're looking at the BUSINESS behind the stock. Like buying a shop — you'd
check if it makes money, how much debt it has, who runs it.

Standard metrics used worldwide:

EARNINGS & PROFITABILITY
------------------------
- Revenue / Sales         — How much money is the company making?
- Net Profit              — What's left after all expenses?
- EPS (Earnings Per Share) — Profit divided by number of shares
- P/E Ratio (Price to Earnings) — Stock price divided by EPS.
                            Low P/E = "cheap", High P/E = "expensive" (roughly)
- PEG Ratio               — P/E divided by growth rate. Adjusts for how fast
                            the company is growing
- EBITDA                   — Earnings before interest, tax, depreciation,
                            amortization. Shows operating performance
- Operating Margin         — What % of revenue is actual operating profit
- Net Profit Margin        — What % of revenue is final profit

BALANCE SHEET (HEALTH)
----------------------
- Debt to Equity Ratio    — How much borrowed money vs own money.
                            High = risky, Low = safe
- Current Ratio           — Can the company pay its short-term bills?
                            Above 1 = yes, Below 1 = trouble
- Book Value Per Share    — If you sold everything and paid all debts,
                            what's each share worth?
- P/B Ratio (Price to Book) — Stock price vs book value.
                            Below 1 = trading below its asset value

CASH FLOW
---------
- Free Cash Flow (FCF)    — Cash left after running the business and
                            investing. This is REAL money, harder to fake
- Cash Flow from Operations — Cash generated from actual business,
                            not investments or loans
- DCF (Discounted Cash Flow) — We covered this. Future cash flows
                            discounted to today's value

RETURNS & EFFICIENCY
--------------------
- ROE (Return on Equity)  — How much profit from shareholders' money.
                            Above 15% = generally good
- ROCE (Return on Capital Employed) — How efficiently is ALL capital
                            (equity + debt) being used?
- ROA (Return on Assets)  — How much profit from total assets

GROWTH
------
- Revenue Growth Rate     — Is the company growing? How fast?
- EPS Growth Rate         — Are profits per share increasing?
- Same Store Sales Growth — For retail: are existing stores selling more?

VALUATION
---------
- P/E Ratio               — Most common. "Am I paying too much per rupee
                            of earnings?"
- EV/EBITDA               — Enterprise Value to EBITDA. Better than P/E
                            because it accounts for debt
- P/S Ratio (Price to Sales) — Used for companies not yet profitable
                            (startups, high-growth)
- Dividend Yield          — Annual dividend as % of stock price


TECHNICAL ANALYSIS — "When should I buy or sell?"
=================================================

You're looking at PRICE AND VOLUME patterns on charts. You don't care what
the company does — you care what the market is doing with the stock.

PRICE-BASED INDICATORS
-----------------------
- Moving Averages (MA)    — Average price over a period (10-day, 50-day,
                            200-day). Shows the trend direction.
                            Price above MA = uptrend. Below = downtrend.
- EMA (Exponential MA)    — Same as MA but gives more weight to recent
                            prices. Reacts faster.
- Golden Cross            — When short-term MA crosses ABOVE long-term MA.
                            Bullish signal (Gordon uses this).
- Death Cross             — When short-term MA crosses BELOW long-term MA.
                            Bearish signal.
- Support                 — Price level where stock keeps bouncing UP from.
                            Like a floor.
- Resistance              — Price level where stock keeps falling DOWN from.
                            Like a ceiling.
- Breakout                — When price pushes through resistance.
                            Gordon's key signal.
- 52-Week High/Low        — Highest and lowest price in last year.
                            Breaking 52-week high = strong momentum.

VOLUME-BASED INDICATORS
------------------------
- Volume                  — Number of shares traded. High volume confirms
                            price moves. Low volume = weak/unreliable move.
- Delivery Volume         — Shares actually transferred to buyer's account
                            (not just intraday traded). Gordon specifically
                            tracks this — it shows real conviction.
- OBV (On Balance Volume) — Running total of volume on up vs down days.
                            Rising OBV = accumulation. Falling = distribution.
- Volume Spike            — Sudden jump in trading volume. Gordon's first
                            filter: weekly volume 5x annual average.

MOMENTUM INDICATORS
--------------------
- RSI (Relative Strength Index) — 0 to 100 scale. Above 70 = overbought
                            (might fall). Below 30 = oversold (might rise).
- MACD                    — Moving Average Convergence Divergence. Shows
                            momentum shifts. Signal line crossover = trade signal.
- Relative Strength       — How a stock performs vs the overall market.
                            Outperforming = strong. Gordon uses this.
- Stochastic Oscillator   — Similar to RSI but compares closing price to
                            price range over a period.

CHART PATTERNS (Gordon uses these specifically)
-----------------------------------------------
- Cup and Handle          — Price drops, rounds out, comes back up (the cup),
                            small dip (handle), then breaks out. Bullish.
- Inverted Head & Shoulders — Three bottoms, middle one deepest. When price
                            breaks above the "neckline" = reversal signal.
- Darvas Box              — Stock trades in a tight range (box). When it
                            breaks above the box on volume = buy signal.
                            Nicolas Darvas invented this in the 1950s.
- Minervini Volatility    — Stock's price range gets tighter and tighter
  Contraction Pattern       (volatility shrinks). Like a spring compressing.
  (VCP)                     When it breaks out = explosive move.
                            Mark Minervini popularized this.
- Head & Shoulders        — Opposite of inverted. Three peaks, middle one
                            highest. Breaking neckline = sell signal.
                            Gordon uses this as an EXIT signal.
- Consolidation           — Price moves sideways in a range for 6+ months.
                            Gordon's key setup — breakout from long
                            consolidation = something has changed.

CANDLESTICK PATTERNS
---------------------
- Long Upper Wick         — Price went up during the day/week but sellers
                            pushed it back down. Signals selling pressure.
                            Gordon watches for 3-4 weeks of these as EXIT signal.
- Doji                    — Open and close nearly equal. Market is undecided.
- Engulfing Pattern       — One candle completely covers the previous one.
                            Signals reversal.

MARKET BREADTH (Gordon uses these)
-----------------------------------
- Advance/Decline Ratio   — How many stocks going up vs down on a given day.
                            More advancing = healthy market.
- 52-Week Highs vs Lows   — How many stocks hitting new highs vs new lows.
                            Tells you where the market strength really is.
- Sector Rotation         — Which sectors are leading and which are lagging.
                            Money moves from sector to sector in cycles.


GORDON'S APPROACH — COMBINING BOTH
====================================

Step 1: Technical signals FIRST (volume spike, breakout, moving averages)
        → "Something is happening with this stock"

Step 2: Fundamental check SECOND (revenue growth, margin change, debt reduction)
        → "Here's WHY something is happening"

Step 3: Technical signals for EXIT (upper wicks, head & shoulders, MA breaks)
        → "The party is over, time to leave"

Most investors do it the other way — find a good company first, then check
the chart. Gordon flips it: find unusual price/volume activity first, then
check if the fundamentals support it. That's his edge.
